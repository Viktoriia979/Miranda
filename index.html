Miranda:

Miranda is one of the natural satellites of Uranus and is the smallest of Uranus's five major moons. Scientists study Miranda because it has one of the most unusual surfaces in the Solar System. Its cliffs, valleys, and ridges help scientists understand how moons change over time and how geological activity can reshape a moon's surface.

Scientists believe Miranda formed about 4.5 billion years ago from the cloud of gas, dust, and ice that surrounded Uranus as the planet was forming. It is made mainly of water ice, mixed with rock and small amounts of frozen gases. Some scientists think Miranda may have been broken apart by a massive collision long ago and then pulled back together by its own gravity, giving it its unusual appearance.

Miranda has a rough and rocky icy surface with giant cliffs, deep valleys, and large ridges. One of its most famous features is Verona Rupes, which is one of the tallest known cliffs in the Solar System. Miranda has a diameter of about 472 kilometres, making it much smaller than Earth's Moon. It orbits Uranus at a distance of about 130,000 kilometres and takes about 1.4 Earth days to complete one orbit.
